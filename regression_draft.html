Explaining determinants on why and when Governments adopt containment measures against COVID-19¶

a preliminary study assessing the determinants of the adoption of nation-level NPI policies against the spread of COVID-19
_non-pharmaceutical-intervention_

30/03/2020, Rome (Italy)

By the time we are writing the recent global virus called COVID-19 has hit more than 190 countries and governments all over the globe have adopted (or are about to adopt) with different timing containment measures in order to limit the devastating effects it is causing to the economy and to people’s health. The disease has already reached a pandemic scale but still many governments hesitate taking action despite it is now clear to all that early or preventive measures can effectively limit the exponential spread.
Policy interventions include a variety of instruments and most of them imply citizens’ liberties restrictions and this appears to be one of the main drivers refraining governments from intervening. Moreover, besides social and political implications, those tools have also different relative costs and they could be not available for some countries.
A preventive intervention is key when dealing with these kind of diseases because it mainly allows to flatten the epidemiological curve in order to reduce the infection rate (and consequently losses in human lives) and also manage better the emergency preventing the healthcare systems from overload. The timing of this anticipated intervention is crucial for obtaining better results, as the example figure below shows, anticipation of the policy might obtain greater results in terms of reducing the overall losses created by the exponential spread of the epidemic (see red and green line).

But while some governments have been prudent in adopting containment policies, others have immediately taken actions. We study the determinants that should lead government to take more immediate actions over different political and socio-economic sphere, taking into account several considerations suggested by the context:

  • Since the clinical consequences of COVID-19 have been found to be more arsh with respect to older population (in particular those aged over 65), countries with an higher fraction of older population should be more concerned, and thus should act faster.
  • When deciding on the adoption of NPI policies, a trade off between liberty of citizen and public health concerns emerges, thus country with higher "civil liberties" should have more difficulties in adopting constrainment policies.
  • The government needs "data" to justifies containment measures, an higher number of certified confirmed cases and deaths related to covid-19 should ease the adoption of NPI while convincing population to accept them.
  • Countries with a more developed Health System can have more toleration on the spread of COVID-19
  • media coverage and public opinion could press the government to act.
  • strong relationship with affected countries could increase risk of spread and enhance NPI adoption

we also control for peculiar country economic situation measured with GDP per capita, as well the "world evolution" of the spread with time fix effects.

MODELLING FRAMEWORK¶

In order to estimate the incidence and the statistical significance of the factor associated with policy adoption we made use of "survival analysis"/"Event history Analysis" framework. Our econometric approach uses a cox-time varying regression, where our left-hand variable (the event) is a dummy between 0 and 1 wether the policy has been adopted or not at time t.

thebaseline component b0(t) will capture all the world events that vary over time and are not specific to country i, while the xi(t) are our coovariates (i.e. the factors associated with policy adoption), with Bi(t) the coefficients expressing the magnitude of the association.

RESULTS¶

We estimate two models for different policy options (the closure of schools and lockdownd policy), and we report the coefficients and the statistical test associated for relevant variable of interest:

  • the share of people over 65 years old (_share_65plus)
  • the number of confirmed and deaths cases due to COVID-19 (_confirmedcases & _deathscases)
  • the level of civil liberties (CiviLiberties)
  • the level of attention of the media related to COVID-19 (trend)
  • the level of the Health System measures with health expenditure (_health_expenditure_per_capitaPPP)

we further control for the level of income (_GDPcapita), the geographic location to account for the potential spread of the virus due to climate (LON), the potential spread due to relationship with other countries (_GLOBINDEX). Our modelling framework also take into account the fact that the two policy options are "incremental" (i.e. shutdown normally comes after closure of schools), thus, when we estimate the model we also control for the adoption of other containment policies (i.e. we control for closure of large events in the "school closing" model, and we control for school closure in the "lockdown model")

def arrange_dataset(dep_var,dep_var_dummy,lista_covariate,control_variable):
    '''
    PARTE COX REGRESSION
    '''
    dep_var='SCHOOL_CLOSING'
    nome_evento=dep_var_dummy
    ## dataset con gli eventi
    # selezionare solo i paesi censiti  
    DF=DATASET_COMPLETE.loc[DATASET_COMPLETE['ISO3'].isin(country_analized),:].copy()
    # # rimettere variabile time come pdtimedate
    # # fare gli start and stop (con day - 1)  
    DF['stop']=pd.to_datetime(DF['time'])
    DF['start']=DF['stop']-pd.offsets.Day(1)

    country_adopter=list(DF.loc[DF[nome_evento]==1,'ISO3'].unique())
    DF_REGRESSION=pd.DataFrame()

    for paese in DF['ISO3'].unique():
        if paese in country_adopter:
            data_evento=DF.loc[(DF['ISO3']==paese)&(DF[nome_evento]==1),'stop'].min()
            DF_REGRESSION=DF_REGRESSION.append(DF.loc[(DF['ISO3']==paese)&(DF['stop']<=data_evento),:])
        else:
            DF_REGRESSION=DF_REGRESSION.append(DF.loc[(DF['ISO3']==paese),:])
    ### trasforma var in int
    DF_REGRESSION['start']=DF_REGRESSION['start'].apply(lambda x:x.timestamp())
    DF_REGRESSION['stop']=DF_REGRESSION['stop'].apply(lambda x:x.timestamp())

    lista_covariate.extend(control_variable)
    lista_covariate.extend(['start','stop',nome_evento,'ISO3'])

    trend_nulli=DF_REGRESSION.loc[DF_REGRESSION['trend'].isnull(),'time'].min()
    slider_trend_null=(pd.to_datetime(DF_REGRESSION['time'])<trend_nulli)
    DF_REG_NONNULL=DF_REGRESSION.loc[slider_trend_null,lista_covariate]
    ### make percentage in 100 units to facilitate plot interpretation
    DF_REG_NONNULL['share_65_plus']=DF_REG_NONNULL['share_65_plus']*100
    return DF_REG_NONNULL,trend_nulli

Comments of the results¶

We showed results for two differents type of models, in the first the dependent variable is the adoption of "school closing", while in the second model the dependent variable is "lockdown".
The analysis of the coefficient, together with the test on their statistical significance tell us that:

Model 1¶

  • in the first place, the adoption of school closing is positively associated with the vulnerability of the population, represented by the share of over 65 aged people, the "hazard rate" of adopting school closure increases of 13 per cent for each percentage point of the share of over 65 citizens.
  • Countries that score better on the civil liberties index, are less likely, ceteris paribus, of adopting school closures, with a coefficients statistically significant (p-value less than 0.03), with a unit increases of the civil liberties index (between 0 and 1) the hazard rate decreases of 23 per cent (1-0.77).
  • The adoption of containment policy regarding cancellation of large events (EVENT_dummy), is positively associated with the likelihood of closing schools.
  • The number of reported confirmed and deaths cases due to COVID-19 are not statistically significant, implying that countries choose to cloose schools indipendently from the absolute level of cases officilaly documented.

Model 2¶

  • In Model 2, when considering the adoption of lockdown, both civil liberties and the share of over 65, are not statistically significant, this might by explained by the fact that we control for the fact that the country has already adopted school closure and cancellation of large events. Since those policies are "incremental", those factors are already took into considerations and do not matters for the consequent reinforcemnt of containment measures (from school closing to total lockdown).
  • On the other hand, the number of confirmed and deaths cases become statistically significant, suggesting that given the worsening of the epidemic curves, the government chooses to adopt more strict measures. Also the "media pressure" as measured by the google trend index is positively and statistically significant associated with the adoption of lockdown measures.

Final comments¶

The empirical correlations reported in our baselines models are coherent with almost all of the intuitions discussed in the introduction, execpt for the level of health expenditure that is never statistically significant. Regarding first containment measures (such as school closing), the level of civil liberties and the potential vulnerability of the countries is associated with an higher likelihood of adoption of those policies, while actual evolution of reported cases is not influential. While in a second moment, when deciding on more stricter measures such as complete shutdown, the actual evloution of the spread of the epidemic matters.
